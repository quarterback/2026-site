How I Work

People ask what I actually do all day. The short version is on the front page: I find systems that have stopped making sense — to the people inside them and the people they’re meant to serve — and I make them legible enough to fix. Here’s the longer version.

The work, in four moves

  1. 01

    Find the messy system

    I go where the work actually happens — shadowing, interviewing, reading the policy and the workarounds side by side. The gap between how something is documented and how it really runs is usually where the problem is hiding. I don’t trust the org chart; I trust the spreadsheet someone keeps on their desktop to make the real job possible.

  2. 02

    Make it legible

    I draw the map: service blueprints, information architecture, the journeys and the handoffs nobody owns. Most dysfunction isn’t a people problem — it’s an invisibility problem. Once everyone can see the same system at the same time, the argument stops being about blame and starts being about the fix.

  3. 03

    Build what’s missing

    Then I build the missing piece — a prototype, a process, a tool, sometimes a team. Forward-deployed means I’m in it with you, shipping something real, not handing over a deck and a calendar invite. I’d rather put a rough working thing in front of people this week than a polished plan next quarter.

  4. 04

    Keep it moving

    And I stay long enough to leave it running without me — documentation, governance, and people who can carry it after I’m gone. The job isn’t finished when it works once; it’s finished when someone else can keep it working. Repair over disruption.

What that looks like in practice

  • Discovery & research

    Interviews, observation, and document archaeology to surface how a service really behaves — not how it’s described in the deck.

  • Service design

    Blueprints, journey maps, and the unglamorous handoffs between teams, channels, and systems where things quietly break.

  • Information architecture

    Structure, language, and content strategy so people can find what they need and trust what they find.

  • Prototyping & delivery

    Building working artifacts — flows, tools, pilots — and shipping them in short cycles instead of long plans.

  • Governance & operations

    The rules, roles, and rituals that keep a thing running and improving once the project lights go off.

  • Teaching & facilitation

    Workshops, studios, and coursework — including teaching service and interaction design at Taubman College — so teams leave more capable than I found them.

How I tend to engage

  • Embedded, not adjacent

    I work inside the team, in the open, on the real problem — not from the outside with a separate roadmap.

  • Short cycles

    Small, visible moves that earn the next one. Momentum beats a perfect plan that never ships.

  • Plain language

    If I can’t explain the system to the people who live in it, I don’t understand it yet.

  • Leave it better

    Success is a team that can keep going without me. I’m trying to work myself out of the job.

What I believe

  • Repair over disruption.
  • Legibility comes before solutions.
  • Design has consequences — name them out loud.
  • Build for the public, and build it in the open.
  • The work isn’t done until someone else can do it.
