## Why I did this

When 18F shut down, a lot of people started asking big questions about civic tech — [whether what was missing was permission to break the rules and bulldoze the bureaucracy](https://blog.ronbronson.com/on-public-mechanics-or-why-im-done-talking-about-civic-tech). I had a smaller, more personal question: can a community of practitioners self-organize around service delivery without a federal mandate, a budget line, or anyone's permission?

I [launched the Digital Corps at the end of March](https://blog.ronbronson.com/civic-tech-local-impact-launching-the-portland-digital-corps) as a short-term experiment. I expected maybe 20 people. Over 100 signed up.

## What we actually did

Fifty-plus designers, developers, strategists, and data people rebuilt six nonprofit websites. Built Oregon, OSU Uplift Lab, Families for Safe Streets Oregon, Cape Perpetua Collaborative — organizations doing important work with digital presences that didn't reflect it.

![Portland Digital Corps website](/img/digitalcorpspdx.org.png)

The format was deliberately stripped down. Small cross-functional teams paired with a nonprofit partner for a focused sprint. No multi-month discovery phase. No 80-page strategy deck. Just: what does this organization need its website to do, and how fast can we get there?

I emphasized at every steering meeting that this was an experiment. That framing mattered — it gave people permission to try things, and it meant we didn't have to pretend we had all the answers.

## What actually happened

The websites shipped. That was the expected part. What I didn't anticipate was the community effect. People kept showing up — not because rebuilding a nonprofit site is exciting, but because they wanted to work alongside people they wouldn't normally meet. Designers next to data engineers. Strategists next to developers. The cross-pollination was the draw, not the deliverable.

Groups from other cities started reaching out, asking how to replicate it. That told me something I've been thinking about ever since: the demand for collaborative, low-ego service work vastly exceeds the supply of places to do it. Most people who care about public service don't have a path to it. They just need someone to [hold the door open](https://blog.ronbronson.com/building-a-career-by-holding-the-door-open).

## What it taught me

Looking back, I wrote that it was ["a rewarding community-centered project that I'm glad I launched without thinking about it too much."](https://blog.ronbronson.com/wrapped-up-the-pdx-digital-corps-experiment-this-week) That's still true, and the "without thinking about it too much" part is the lesson.

The overhead I assumed was necessary — procurement, approvals, formal partnerships, a fully baked plan — turned out to be optional. No budget. No office. No institutional backing. Six websites shipped. The volunteers from the first cohort asked when the next one starts.

I keep coming back to this project because it proved something the [public mechanics](https://blog.ronbronson.com/on-public-mechanics-or-why-im-done-talking-about-civic-tech) frame predicts: the path forward isn't one big fix. It's a lot of small, durable, iterative things. And the people willing to do them are already out there — they just need the on-ramp.
