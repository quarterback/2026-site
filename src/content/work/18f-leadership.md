## What I walked into

I arrived at 18F in 2016 thinking I understood how government worked. I didn't. I understood how *projects* worked. Government isn't projects — it's procurement cycles that outlast any roadmap, hiring freezes that arrive without warning, political transitions that rewrite priorities overnight. The org chart reflects a political reality, not a design logic.

18F was supposed to be the startup inside government. In some ways it was. But the interesting question was never "how do we work around the constraints?" It was "what does good look like *inside* them?"

## The people

[What people misunderstand about public service](https://blog.ronbronson.com/some-thoughts-on-hiring-quality-public-service) is the sheer quality and commitment of the people who choose it. The systems fail them, not the other way around. When I became Head of Design, the practice had 21 people. I grew it to over 40 — but the headcount was never the point. The point was figuring out what design actually *means* in a federal context, which turns out to be a different discipline than private-sector product design.

In tech, design leadership is about shipping. In government, it's about building capacity — teaching partner agencies to do the work after you leave. That changes what you hire for, how you run critique, how you define success. I built hiring processes that worked within 6–12 month federal timelines. Career frameworks that treated government design as its own thing, not a lesser version of something else. Feedback systems that balanced delivery with growth.

None of this was glamorous. Most of it was spreadsheets, one-on-ones, and conversations that took months to land.

The thing I'm proudest of: the designers I hired went on to lead practices at the VA, CMS, USDS, and state and local agencies across the country. The practice outlived the organization.

## The visibility problem

The deeper I got into leading teams — at 18F, in teaching, in coaching — the more I kept running into the same breakdown. [Delivery is a visibility problem.](https://blog.ronbronson.com/delivery-is-a-visibility-problem) The frameworks we use to ship things assume that shared context will naturally emerge. It doesn't. Someone sees a performance issue, someone else sees a scope problem, leadership sees a timeline risk — everyone's solving a different problem, and nobody realizes it until the retrospective.

The best teams I saw at 18F could do three things: describe what they were doing and why, question assumptions without fear, and recover quickly when something changed. The struggling teams had plenty of talent. What they lacked was the ability to see the same picture at the same time.

As Chief of Staff at TTS — the parent org of 18F, login.gov, and cloud.gov — this became the whole job. Translating between technical teams and political leadership. Managing communications during uncertainty. Coordinating strategy across products with different funding models, different constituencies, different definitions of success. Not design work in the traditional sense. But the same core skill: making the situation legible to everyone who needs to act on it.

## The hard lesson

You can build something excellent inside an institution and still watch it get dismantled for reasons that have nothing to do with quality. 18F closed. The work didn't disappear — it lives in the agencies it changed, the people it trained, the practices that became standard. But the institution couldn't survive the politics.

That taught me something I keep coming back to: institutions remember practices, not people. The brilliant solo contribution gets forgotten. The hiring process, the critique protocol, the way you documented things — those survive. If you want lasting impact, build things other people can maintain when you leave.

Eight years taught me that making institutions work differently is slow, unglamorous, and deeply political. It's also — and I didn't expect this when I started — the most important work I've done.
